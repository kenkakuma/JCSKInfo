Blue Origin has revealed its plans for an [orbital AI data center](https://www.engadget.com/ai/orbital-ai-data-centers-could-work-but-they-might-ruin-earth-in-the-process-170000099.html) system in a new [filing](https://fccprod.servicenowservices.com/icfs?id=ibfs_application_summary&number=SAT-LOA-20260310-00118) with the Federal Communications Commission. The company has asked the agency for permission to deploy 51,600 satellites, as reported by the [_Wall Street Journal_](https://shopping.yahoo.com/rdlw?merchantId=2f007401-3eaa-4237-b69b-54ccbe125502&siteId=us-engadget&pageId=1p-autolink&contentUuid=7a9792d1-55b2-4031-a177-bd8afb35e144&featureId=text-link&merchantName=The+Wall+Street+Journal&linkText=Wall+Street+Journal&custData=eyJzb3VyY2VOYW1lIjoiV2ViLURlc2t0b3AtVmVyaXpvbiIsImxhbmRpbmdVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy53c2ouY29tL3RlY2gvYmx1ZS1vcmlnaW4tZm9ybWFsbHktZW50ZXJzLXJhY2UtdG8tZGV2ZWxvcC1kYXRhLWNlbnRlcnMtaW4tc3BhY2UtZDdmZWZhMDA_c3Q9dnp6VnFLJnJlZmxpbms9ZGVza3RvcHdlYnNoYXJlX3Blcm1hbGluayIsImNvbnRlbnRVdWlkIjoiN2E5NzkyZDEtNTViMi00MDMxLWExNzctYmQ4YWZiMzVlMTQ0Iiwib3JpZ2luYWxVcmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy53c2ouY29tL3RlY2gvYmx1ZS1vcmlnaW4tZm9ybWFsbHktZW50ZXJzLXJhY2UtdG8tZGV2ZWxvcC1kYXRhLWNlbnRlcnMtaW4tc3BhY2UtZDdmZWZhMDA_c3Q9dnp6VnFLJnJlZmxpbms9ZGVza3RvcHdlYnNoYXJlX3Blcm1hbGluayJ9&signature=AQAAATlvidpicRJVMHwLkCN0NI7GdQBBwrR6Mf-eSltQmlbb&gcReferrer=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.wsj.com%2Ftech%2Fblue-origin-formally-enters-race-to-develop-data-centers-in-space-d7fefa00%3Fst%3DvzzVqK%26reflink%3Ddesktopwebshare_permalink) and [_SpaceNews_](https://spacenews.com/blue-origin-joins-the-orbital-data-center-race/). Called Project Sunrise, the initiative aims to launch and operate a constellation of satellites that can deliver computing capacity for artificial intelligence uses.

Project Sunrise’s satellites will be placed in sun-synchronous orbits at altitudes between 311 and 1,118 miles. Each layer in the constellation will have between 300 to 1,000 satellites and will be approximately 3 to 6 miles apart. In its filing, Blue Origin said the constellation would complement terrestrial data centers.

The satellites will, of course, will be fitted with solar panels to be able to gather energy from the sun. Blue Origin explained that the orbital AI data center will lower the “marginal cost of compute capacity compared to terrestrial alternatives,” because the satellites will be powered by the sun, won’t need land and won’t need grid infrastructure. Project Sunrise will “enable US companies developing and using AI to flourish, accelerating breakthroughs in machine learning, autonomous systems and predictive analytics,” Blue Origin added.

By filing its request with the FCC, Blue Origin has officially joined SpaceX in the list of companies looking to build an AI data center in space. In January, [SpaceX asked the FCC](https://www.engadget.com/science/space/spacex-wants-to-launch-a-constellation-of-a-million-satellites-to-power-ai-needs-175607771.html) for permission to deploy 1 million satellites for its constellation. The company justified at the time that “orbital data centers are the most efficient way to meet the accelerating demand for AI computing power.”
